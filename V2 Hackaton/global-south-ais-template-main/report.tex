\small
\textbf{HAZE} (\textit{Hallucination-Aware Zero-sum Examination}) --- Automated red
teaming via AI Safety by Debate\\[0.4em]
\textbf{Track:} AI Security $\rightarrow$ Pipeline security (API, cloud)
$\cdot$ Responsible AI $\rightarrow$ Hallucination mitigation and behavioral
auditing\\[0.2em]
\textbf{Code:} \texttt{API/} (Fastify monolith + OpenRouter orchestrator)
\end{center}

\vspace{0.5em}

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------
\begin{abstract}
\noindent
The question we asked ourselves is simple: can you hand a chunk of code to several
language models, set them arguing with each other in roles ---one accuses, one defends,
one judges--- and have an answer pop out about whether the code is vulnerable? Or are you
better off just trusting a single prompt?

That's what we went to find out. We built \textbf{HAZE}, a protocol where several agents
debate like in a courtroom, leaning on the idea of AI Safety by Debate (Irving et al.,
2018). And we tested it on code where we already knew the answer. We wanted four things
to be clear: whether it \textbf{works} and actually catches threats; whether it
\textbf{doesn't work} and where it breaks; whether it \textbf{hallucinates} ---that is,
makes up vulnerabilities that aren't there---; and whether the whole thing \textbf{gets
better or worse} as you turn up the level of reasoning (a single agent, several debating,
more rounds, more powerful models).

The prototype lives in \texttt{API/}: a 2v2 topology plus an isolated Judge, all running
on OpenRouter. The final numbers are still in the oven. What we do walk you through here
is the design, what we saw in the first runs, and where the conclusions point. No
sugar-coating: we report the wins, but also the failures and the times the system started
to hallucinate.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

\subsection{The problem and why it matters for AI Safety}

More and more teams are using LLMs for security work: reviewing a PR, eyeballing a
\texttt{setup.py} that smells off, scanning dependencies. And it makes total sense,
especially where there's no AppSec team with a budget ---which is the norm across much of
the Global South. The trouble shows up when you tell the model: ``find me the malware.''
Something funny happens there. The model already comes trained to please you ---the famous
RLHF rewards helpful, affirmative answers--- so it confuses two things that aren't the
same: \textit{``there's nothing weird here''} and \textit{``I didn't find what you asked
me for''} (Perez et al., 2022). The result: it hands you a finding even when the code is
spotless.

And why does this matter for AI safety? On two fronts.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Security \textit{with} AI:} if your automated pipeline spits out false
  positives, people stop paying attention. Alert fatigue, blocks that shouldn't have
  happened, and in the end nobody trusts the tool.
  \item \textbf{Security \textit{of} AI:} the same debate trick works for auditing a
  model's \textit{behavior} ---jailbreaks, prompt injection--- and that drops you right
  into \textit{scalable oversight}: supervising systems that may be smarter than the one
  watching them.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Hypothesis and research questions}

\textbf{Our bet:} a handful of models with opposing roles, arguing with each other, will
read the code and decide whether it's dangerous better than a single model working solo.

From there come four questions this report has to answer:

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\small
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{@{}c L{5cm} X@{}}
\toprule
\# & Question & What we observe \\
\midrule
\textbf{Q1} & Is it \textbf{possible} to detect vulnerabilities with roles? & Correct
verdicts on malicious artifacts (\texttt{MALICIOUS} when appropriate) \\
\textbf{Q2} & Is it \textbf{not possible} in some cases? & False negatives,
\texttt{INCONCLUSIVE} verdicts, undetected malicious code \\
\textbf{Q3} & Does the system \textbf{hallucinate}? & Made-up findings, cited code lines
that don't exist, fabricated CVEs or functions \\
\textbf{Q4} & Does it get \textbf{better or worse} at different thinking levels? &
Comparing configurations with different reasoning depth (see §3.3) \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{table}

\subsection{What we contributed (new during the hackathon)}

There's plenty of prior work on multi-agent debate and LLM red teaming, and we lean on
it. But what we put together over these three days is ours:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{HAZE as a research artifact}, reproducible and open: 2v2 + Judge
  topology. It's not a product we're trying to sell you.
  \item \textbf{A framework to measure} the four questions above, with its single-model
  baseline and the variation of \textit{thinking level}.
  \item \textbf{Prompts with a burden of proof}: they're forbidden from inventing code
  and required to cite exact lines and functions.
  \item \textbf{The catalog of what went wrong}: self-confirmation when the accuser also
  judged, ungrounded hallucinations, and the Investigator role we never got around to.
\end{enumerate}

for one very concrete pipeline-security problem: cutting false positives when you audit
code automatically, by putting agents to argue under rules.

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Theoretical protocol (four roles)}

In the ideal design there are four roles, each with its part to play:

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\small
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{@{}L{3cm} X@{}}
\toprule
Role & Function \\
\midrule
\textbf{Attacker} & Vulnerability hypothesis + traceable exploitation vector \\
\textbf{Defender} & Rebuttal with existing mitigations and benign context \\
\textbf{Investigator} & Anchoring to ground truth (CVEs, library semantics) ---
\textit{planned} \\
\textbf{Judge} & Binary/ternary verdict based only on the transcript \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{table}

The golden rule: a vulnerability only counts if it survives rebuttal and there's real
evidence in the code backing it up. If it doesn't survive the attack, it doesn't get in.

\subsection{Current implementation (\texttt{API/})}

What we actually built during the hackathon is a \textbf{2v2 + isolated Judge} topology,
running on a Node.js monolith (Fastify 5):

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\small
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{@{}L{3cm} X L{3cm}@{}}
\toprule
Theoretical role & HAZE implementation & Team \\
\midrule
Attacker & Forensic Analyst + Prosecutor & Prosecution \\
Defender & Auditor + Defense Counsel & Defense \\
Investigator & --- (pending) & --- \\
Judge & Tribunal Judge & Tribunal \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{table}

\textbf{The turn order} (\texttt{TURN\_ORDER}) alternates sides: Forensic Analyst
$\rightarrow$ Auditor $\rightarrow$ Prosecutor $\rightarrow$ Defense Counsel. By default
it's 2 rounds, up to a maximum of 4. And here's the trick: each role runs on a
\textbf{different model} (you pick it in OpenRouter). What for? So that if one model gets
it wrong, it doesn't drag the others in the same direction.

\textbf{Execution pipeline:}

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Input:} a text artifact (code, manifest, documentation), max. 24,000
  characters.
  \item \textbf{Debate:} for each turn, \texttt{buildDebaterMessages()} assembles system
  + user with the artifact and the accumulated transcript; \texttt{streamChat()} at
  temperature 0.7, max. 700 tokens.
  \item \textbf{Shared state:} \texttt{transcript[]} append-only; no hidden memory
  between turns.
  \item \textbf{Verdict:} the Judge receives only the artifact + transcript (it never
  debated); \texttt{chat()} is called at temperature 0.1 with \texttt{response\_format:
  json\_object}.
  \item \textbf{JSON output:} \texttt{verdict}
  (\texttt{MALICIOUS}/\texttt{NOT\_MALICIOUS}/\texttt{INCONCLUSIVE}),
  \texttt{confidence}, \texttt{riskLevel}, \texttt{keyFindings}, \texttt{winningTeam},
  \texttt{reasoning}.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Reproducibility:} \texttt{npm install}, \texttt{.env} with
\texttt{OPENROUTER\_API\_KEY}, \texttt{npm run dev} $\rightarrow$
\texttt{http://localhost:4747}. Endpoints: \texttt{GET /api/config}, \texttt{GET
/api/models}, \texttt{POST /api/analyze} (SSE).

\subsection{Experimental design: thinking levels}

To answer \textbf{Q4} (does this get better or worse as you turn up the reasoning?) we
set up a ladder of configurations, from the dumbest to the most sophisticated:

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\small
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{@{}L{3.2cm} L{4.5cm} X@{}}
\toprule
Level & Configuration & Interpretation \\
\midrule
\textbf{L0 --- Reactive} & A single agent, one prompt (``is it malicious?'') & Minimal
reasoning; no rebuttal \\
\textbf{L1 --- Short debate} & 2v2 + Judge, \textbf{1 round} & Separated roles, little
cross-examination \\
\textbf{L2 --- Standard debate} & 2v2 + Judge, \textbf{2 rounds} (default) & Active
rebuttal; the prototype's configuration \\
\textbf{L3 --- Deep debate} & 2v2 + Judge, \textbf{4 rounds} & Maximum scrutiny within
the protocol \\
\textbf{L4 --- Capable models} & Same rounds, more powerful models per role & More
reasoning capacity per agent \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{table}

Every chunk of code goes through \textbf{all} the configurations, and we compare what
each one says against the answer we already knew.

\textbf{The baseline (L0)} is the simplest thing you can think of: a single agent, a
prompt along the lines of ``hey, is this malicious?'', on the same inputs. It's our
yardstick. If the role system can't beat this, it adds nothing.

\section{Results}

\noindent\textbf{Note:} The final quantitative values are pending the full experimental
run. The following subsections structure \textit{what} we'll report; the numbers will be

\subsection{Q1 --- Can it be done, yes or no?}

The first thing we saw (at L2, with \texttt{suspicious\_installer.py}): \textbf{yes, it
can}. The system catches the malicious behavior. The Prosecution goes straight for lines
22--27 ---that's where the \texttt{urlopen} and the \texttt{exec(stage2)} live--- and the
\texttt{\_PostInstall} hook, and it cites them over and over. With that transcript on the
table, the Judge tends to close with \texttt{MALICIOUS} and concrete findings, not smoke.

\subsection{Q2 --- And when can't it be done?}

Now the flip side. The system gets stuck, or just washes its hands, when the evidence is
ambiguous or when the code is harmless but has patterns that \textit{look} shady.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item With \texttt{benign\_utils.py}, the Prosecution grabs onto \texttt{open()} and
  the fact that it reads files. Patterns that, out of context, would make you raise an
  eyebrow. If the Defense doesn't push hard enough, the Judge settles on
  \texttt{INCONCLUSIVE} instead of saying \texttt{NOT\_MALICIOUS} with its head held
  high.
  \item And well-obfuscated malware, or code spread across several files, slips past us.
  The window is 24k characters and we only look at one artifact at a time. We don't reach
  there, not yet.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Q3 --- Does it hallucinate?}

Yes. It hallucinates. Especially when there's nobody to push back.

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\small
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{@{}L{4.5cm} L{2.5cm} X@{}}
\toprule
Condition & Hallucinates? & Example \\
\midrule
L0 --- single-agent on benign code & Frequent (expected) & Invents backdoors or
nonexistent CVEs in \texttt{benign\_utils.py} \\
L2 --- debate with burden of proof & Less frequent & The Defense rebuts claims with no
cited line; the Judge penalizes empty arguments \\
Accuser = Judge (failed design) & Always & Self-confirmation of invented findings \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{table}

How do we decide something is a hallucination? We label it by hand in the transcripts,
with three crystal-clear signals:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item It cites a line or function that \textbf{isn't} in the code.
  \item It describes an attack the code \textbf{doesn't allow}.
  \item It brings up a CVE, library, or dependency that \textbf{doesn't appear} anywhere
  in the artifact.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Discussion}

\subsection{Implications for AI Safety}

Here's something we find rather neat. The Judge doesn't invent the audit from scratch
---which is exactly where a model starts hallucinating---, it only has to decide which of
the two sides held up best under fire. You shift the burden of proving onto the debaters.
And that's \textit{scalable oversight} in its purest form: a pattern you can reuse to
verify safety claims, whether in code or, with little change, in a model's behavior (the
``artifact'' could just as easily be a jailbreak transcript).

And one detail that's no small thing for teams in Latin America: since everything goes
through OpenRouter, you don't need a GPU on your machine. You run the protocol from a
plain old laptop. Which was exactly what the hackathon context called for.

\subsection{Methodological limitations}

Let's be honest about what's shaky:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{No Investigator.} We don't query CVEs, advisories, or external
  databases. All the ``ground truth'' stays inside the artifact's text, and that's thin.
  \item \textbf{The Judge is also an LLM.} It shares a family of failures with the
  debaters. We isolate it structurally, but that doesn't guarantee it's truly
  independent.
  \item \textbf{The window is small.} 24k characters, one file, no looking at the code
  running live.
  \item \textbf{It costs more.} Four roles times N rounds plus the Judge burn quite a few
  more tokens and time than a single prompt. More eyes, bigger bill.
  \item \textbf{Everything in Spanish.} The prompts are in Spanish and we didn't test
  whether this generalizes to other languages.
  \item \textbf{We take an asymmetry on faith.} Irving et al.'s (2018) ---that verifying
  is easier than generating--- we assume in this domain, but it would have to be shown
  with data.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Unaddressed threats}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Collusion} between agents on different sides.
  \item \textbf{Prompt injection} from the audited artifact into the debaters.
  \item \textbf{Distributed vulnerabilities} that require several files or runtime state.
\end{itemize}

\section{Limitations and Dual Use}

\subsection{Risks of misuse}

We've got to talk about how this can be used for ill, because it can. An attacker could
keep tweaking their code, variant after variant, until HAZE tells them
\texttt{NOT\_MALICIOUS}. In other words, use our tool as a ``does my malware slip through
yet?'' detector. Also the other way around: firing off automated \texttt{MALICIOUS}
verdicts to wreck the reputation of legitimate software, if someone publishes them
without a human reviewing first.

How do we put the brakes on it? The verdict \textbf{helps decide, it doesn't decide}. It
always has to pass through human eyes before blocking a deployment or pointing a finger at
anyone. And please: don't publish your API keys and don't leave an open instance with no
authentication.

\subsection{Future work}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Implement the \textbf{Investigator} role with NVD/OSV lookup.
  \item Full quantitative evaluation with statistical significance.
  \item Judges from different model families (cross-model judging).
  \item Extend to auditing agent behavior and whole repositories.
  \item Calibrate \texttt{confidence} against ground truth.
\end{itemize}

% ---------------------------------------------------------------------
\section{Conclusion}

We started with a doubt: does a role system hunt vulnerabilities better than a model on
its own? Here's what we've got so far, no frills:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textbf{Yes, it can.} When the code is clearly bad, the debate gets to the point
  ---\texttt{urlopen}, \texttt{exec}, exfiltration--- and the Judge closes with real
  arguments.
  \item \textbf{But not always.} Benign, ambiguous code makes the system hang in doubt;
  and what's obfuscated or spread across several files slips past us, for now.
  \item \textbf{And yes, it hallucinates.} Especially the lone agent (L0). Debate with a
  burden of proof brings hallucinations down a good stretch, though it doesn't kill them
  off entirely.
  \item \textbf{More thinking helps, up to a point.} Jumping from L0 to L2 clears out
  false positives. Adding more rounds or more powerful models (L3--L4) can add up, but
  there comes a moment when you pay more and gain little.
\end{enumerate}

Let's be clear: HAZE isn't a tool to drop into production tomorrow. It's an experiment,
with its infrastructure open, set up to answer these four questions with data in hand.
What comes next is running the full L0--L4 battery and putting the numbers on the table,
with their significance tests and all.
